Hi, I'm Saida. I am building FlintShift, an AI governance consultancy firm in Zug serving regulated financial services and SMBs in Switzerland and the EU.

The short version: I've spent 20+ years in banking governance, risk, and compliance (about half of that in internal audit) most recently at a UK broker-dealer's Swiss branch. I'm building FlintShift as a holding company with three product lines: Advisory (assessments and implementation), Academy (training and curriculum), and AI With SAIDA (the consumer-facing brand, hosted by Cleo, a Cleopatra-inspired AI oracle persona). My first paying client is the headline 2026 goal, and I'm coming into this cohort because the right room of builders is the fastest way to keep learning, and because doing it solo isn't as fun as doing it with other like-minded people.

What I'm building

GovAssess. A proprietary 5-domain AI Governance Readiness Assessment for banks and financial institutions navigating the EU AI Act, FINMA AI Guidelines, and Model Risk Management (SR 11-7 / SS1/23).

The Prompt Playbook. A non-digital deliverable for clients in air-gapped environments where API access is prohibited (common in highly regulated industries). Physical artifact, multilingual, hands-on workshop format.

FlintShift Academy. Curriculum assets designed for regulated-industry audiences who can't safely follow generic creator-economy AI training.

Top five skills

  1. Banking and financial services GRC. 20+ years across the regulatory frameworks that actually bind: FINMA, EU AI Act, GDPR/nDSG, SR 11-7, SS1/23 Model Risk Management. Half that time in internal audit, which is where you learn to test what people tell you rather than trust it.
  2. Trust-but-verify engineering, applied to AI. I treat AI agent self-reports as unverified until terminal-confirmed, tag outputs with epistemic confidence (verified / inferred / judgment-required / unknown), and build audit-trail defensibility into the architecture. Habits ported directly from GRC into how I build.
  3. Multilingual delivery into Swiss / EU regulated environments. English, French, German, Italian, Arabic. In-person, in-language, in-the-room delivery is the moat that doesn't get cloned by an offshore competitor.
  4. Cross-jurisdictional perspective. Born in France to Moroccan parents, lived and worked across Switzerland, Hong Kong, Singapore, and London. I read regulatory and cultural context across markets in a way that's hard to fake.
  5. Building AI agent ecosystems with GRC rigour. I run a multi-agent stack (Cleo, the Aurora morning brief, GovAssess, Prompt Architect, AI Boardroom, Oracle, Lumina, Dream Cycle) and I've done the unglamorous infrastructure work: Supabase Edge Function cost instrumentation, VPS-to-Vercel migrations, hardening agent memory layers against the Nate Jones 12 primitives.

Where I'm weaker

I sometimes default to infrastructure and automation building when the uncomfortable work is direct, relationship-based selling. The cohort is partly a forcing function on that.

What's driving me to do this

Two things, and they're connected.

I want to keep upskilling, so going through the Anthropic CCA-F as a group rather than solo is the obvious accelerant. I am also enrolled in another programme from the AI Build Lab (Tyler Fisk & Sara Davison) focusing on building an AI-native OS.

And I want a community. A room of like-minded people to learn alongside and have fun with. After enough solo building, that's the thing that compounds.

What I can bring to the cohort

  • Regulated-industry rigour. If anyone in the cohort is building something that might end up touching EU / US / APAC / Swiss financial services, or another regulated vertical, I can stress-test it against the actual frameworks rather than the imagined ones.
  • GRC discipline applied to agent systems. Epistemic confidence tagging, audit-trail design, verification rituals, retrieval-layer hardening: the boring infrastructure that makes AI defensible in a regulated context.
  • Multilingual Swiss / EU / US / APAC network. Five languages and a network across banking, compliance, and the broader regulatory community.
  • Honest pattern-naming. I'll tell you when I think you're building infrastructure to avoid selling, because I do it myself.

What I'd like from the cohort

  • Bouncing ideas, including the half-formed ones.
  • Real feedback, not polite nodding.
  • Support through the build cycles.
  • Knowledge sharing, both directions.
