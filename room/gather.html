Wispr Flow

Voice dictation software that converts speech to text inside any application, in real time — you speak, it types. Different from Plaud, which is a hardware recorder for later transcription. Wendy uses it as her main input method for prompts and messages and offered the group a referral link for a free month. It came up when someone noted that most of Wendy's prompts are dictated rather than typed, and the quality is high enough that the two are almost indistinguishable.

Knowledge poverty

A term coined in this session for the gap between information that exists and information people can actually access and use when they need it. Like food poverty or time poverty, but applied to knowledge: having too little of the right kind at the right moment to make good decisions. It surfaced in a conversation about AI model costs and the risk that AI improvements remain concentrated in those who can afford them, widening the gap between knowledge-rich and knowledge-poor people and organisations.

Founder OS

A personal AI operating system built around a founder's own context, projects, and working patterns: memory files, scheduled agents, and connected tools that collectively know who you are and what you're working on, so you never have to re-brief from scratch. Wendy described building her version and plans to push the framework to GitHub for the cohort to fork. The idea is to make the AI feel like a collaborator who was in every previous meeting, not a new hire who needs onboarding each time.

The group returned to a principle from earlier sessions: front-load cheap, deterministic steps and reserve expensive model calls for the moments that genuinely need them.

MCP (Model Context Protocol)

The standard that lets an AI model connect to external tools, data sources, and services in real time, rather than having you copy and paste content into a chat window. Came up as one of the harder Anthropic Academy courses — Scott noted it assumes code literacy that earlier courses don't. The API and MCP courses are where the cohort noticeably splits into people who build and people who direct, and the exam is expected to test vocabulary and syntax rather than the ability to write code from scratch.

Deterministic layer

A fixed-rules front-end placed in front of an AI agent for steps that are predictable and repetitive. The rules run without touching the AI at all; only when a case falls outside them does it hand off to the probabilistic model. Marc Pozzuoli described building this for his email-sorting agent: the deterministic layer handles the majority of cases instantly at zero token cost, and the AI handles the edge cases. He updates the rules every few weeks as new patterns emerge. The principle — deterministic first, probabilistic only when needed — is expected to become a standard production pattern.

World model

A live file that tracks your current work context — projects, decisions, commitments, priorities — updated automatically from local files, GitHub, email, and task tools. Scott Jennings runs one on his Mac Studio via a scheduled loop; his CLAUDE.md tells every session to read it first, so he never has to re-brief. The result: Claude already knows who he is and what he is working on before he types a word. A structured, file-based answer to the "starting from scratch every session" problem.

FPGA

Field-Programmable Gate Array: a chip whose logic circuits can be reprogrammed after manufacture. Ian Jones raised it as a direction for truly token-free AI skills — once a workflow is learned well enough, you compile it into deterministic hardware logic that produces the same output every time with zero model involvement. His broader point: monetisation pressure pushes AI providers towards consuming more tokens, so the interesting efficiency work is in finding ways to run reliable, repeated tasks without touching the model at all.

Thread forking

Splitting a conversation at a specific point into two or more separate branches to explore different directions in parallel, then selectively merging the useful threads back. Ian Jones is building an agentic model that does exactly this — you say "fork now" and get multiple independent windows running simultaneously. The problem it solves is context contamination: when one line of reasoning bleeds into another and degrades the quality of both. Not the same as ChatGPT's "option A / option B" feature, which is just a single fork on the app layer.

Forward deployed engineer

A technical role where someone embeds inside a client's business to build and implement AI directly, rather than delivering advice from outside. Anthropic is hiring for this at $300k–$400k. The wider significance: experienced leaders are leaving senior positions at major companies to take individual contributor roles at AI labs — the Workday CTO leaving for a senior developer position at Anthropic was the specific example raised. Wendy noted she is effectively doing this already on a freelance basis, and sees it as one of the biggest near-term opportunities for the cohort.

Plaud

A small clip-on recording device that transcribes speech without needing a phone or screen visible. It also picks up phone calls via vibration conduction — press the button when a call starts and it captures both sides. Wendy is using it to record in-person meetings and conferences where producing a phone would change the room dynamic. Scott noted he has never seen his drop below 50% battery. Key consideration: it captures everything on screen and everything said, so it needs switching off during sensitive or government-contracted work.

Little Bird

A behavioural AI observer that monitors screen activity and reports on working patterns — what you front-load, what you defer, how many tabs you have open, when you go idle. Wendy has been running it for a couple of weeks and noted it picked up that she works mostly by voice dictation rather than typing, that she front-loads her day before the US wakes up, and that she had 21 tabs open at one point. She has a code for a couple of months free if anyone wants to try it. Caution: she is likely switching it off during a local authority contract, since it observes everything on screen.

Buffer

A scheduling tool that lets you queue posts to LinkedIn and other social platforms in advance. You cannot post directly to LinkedIn via API, but you can post to Buffer and Buffer handles the scheduling. Marc Pozzuoli uses it as the final step in his automated LinkedIn pipeline: Claude writes, critiques, rewrites, and humanises the post from meeting transcripts, then pushes it to Buffer, which publishes it at the right time. The only human step is a final read before it goes live.

Hermes agent

An agent runtime that gives Claude a notably large context window — around 40,000 tokens of prompt and tool space. Scott Jennings uses it for scheduled background jobs on his Mac Studio, running the Qwen local model for routine tasks and only calling out to Sonnet 4.6 when heavier synthesis is needed. It came up in the context of the minimum viable model principle: use the smallest capable model for the job, and Hermes gives you room to load tools and context without hitting limits.

Vibe coding

Building software by directing an AI agent through intent and feedback rather than writing code yourself. You describe what you want, review what comes back, redirect, and iterate — the agent does the actual writing. Wendy used it to describe a rescue project she picked up: an accountant at a local firm had vibe-coded a functional app, linked it to external data, and then told no one, left no documentation, and disappeared from the process. The app worked; the governance around it did not. Vibe coding lowers the barrier to building, but also to building things no one else can maintain.

Technical discussions

  • Deterministic agents (Marc Pozzuoli): Marc built an email-sorting agent that started fully probabilistic, learning from how he sorted. When it started burning too many tokens, he had it analyse its own sorting history and write a deterministic front-end — fixed rules for the predictable cases, probabilistic fallback for anything the rules don't cover. He updates the deterministic layer every few weeks as patterns evolve. The result is dramatically lower token usage for routine steps.
  • World model (Scott Jennings): Scott runs a context loop on his Mac Studio that watches his local files, GitHub, ClickUp, and email, and continuously updates a single world model file. Every Claude session reads that file first via CLAUDE.md. He uses Qwen running locally for scheduled jobs (no API cost), and Sonnet 4.6 only for synthesis or cross-document work. His point: minimum capable model for each task — Sonnet is more than enough for chat and co-work, and smarter models applied to simple tasks can actually produce worse output.
  • Thread forking and neurophysiology-inspired AI (Ian Jones OBE): Ian is developing an agentic model that lets you fork a conversation at a chosen point into multiple parallel threads, run them independently, and selectively merge back. He is also researching whether AI responsiveness could be modelled on human neurotransmitter timescales — glutamate for subconscious processing (milliseconds), dopamine (fractions of a second), serotonin (tens of seconds) — to create a layered system that reserves slower, heavier processing for things that genuinely need it.
  • FPGAs (Ian Jones OBE): Ian raised the idea of compiling well-understood AI workflows into FPGA logic gates: deterministic circuits that produce the same output every time with zero token consumption. His broader concern: AI providers are incentivised to maximise token usage because that is their monetisation model, which creates an inherent tension with efficiency. FPGA compilation is one answer to that, though it is currently speculative as a practical tool.
  • LinkedIn automation (Marc Pozzuoli): Marc has fully automated his LinkedIn content. Each week it reviews his meeting transcripts, extracts concepts, obfuscates the people and companies, writes a draft post, critiques and rewrites it through several passes, runs a humanisation step (including removing em dashes), then pushes to Buffer for scheduling. The only human step is a final read before it goes live. One post reached over 20,000 views. He offered to share how it is configured. Wendy noted she wants to do the same for Glassmark.

Token

The unit AI models use to measure and charge for work. Roughly a word, or part of one. When you're told to "watch your usage," this is what you're watching. Came up when Wendy mentioned the Google I/O keynote, where tokens were referenced more often than AI itself, because for the companies building these tools, tokens are what they're selling. Wendy also noted that running scheduled tasks before the US West Coast wakes up cuts both token costs and response times noticeably.

API

Application Programming Interface: the way software talks to other software. Ryan Crabbe walked through the difference between a personal Claude subscription and the API when the group was discussing how businesses actually integrate Claude into their own products. Via the API, you pay per token rather than a flat subscription. Scott added that team and enterprise plans also exist for those who want managed cloud access without building on the API directly.

Vector store

A way of storing information so that an AI can search it by meaning rather than by exact words. If you ask "what did we discuss about costs?" a vector store finds relevant content even if no one used the word "costs" specifically. Think of it as a library organised by concept rather than alphabetical title. Scott introduced the term in the context of IMI, his product that turns meeting transcripts into a searchable knowledge layer.

Knowledge graph

A way of organising information as a web of connected ideas rather than a flat list. Each concept is a node, and the connections between them are the relationships. It helps an AI understand not just facts but how they relate to each other, and lets you filter by proximity to what you're working on right now. Scott's IMI product builds exactly this from meeting transcripts, making forgotten decisions visible and traceable.

Open Brain

A persistent memory layer that sits outside any single AI tool, so your context follows you wherever you go. Instead of re-introducing yourself every time you open a new chat, the AI connects to your Open Brain and already knows what you're working on. Wendy introduced the concept; Scott demonstrated his own version running entirely on local hardware. Ryan Crabbe then floated the idea of a multiplayer version shared across a whole business, with Scott offering to help anyone who wants to explore it.

Local model

An AI model running on your own hardware rather than in a data centre somewhere. No API costs per query, data doesn't leave your machine, and you're not dependent on a provider's uptime or pricing. The trade-off: setup is more involved, and your hardware sets the ceiling on capability. Ian Jones and Scott made the case for local models when the group got into a discussion about cost, privacy and data sovereignty, though the question of backup strategy was left open.

Frontier model

The most capable, most expensive AI models at the cutting edge: Claude, GPT-4o, Gemini Ultra. Ryan Crabbe raised this when arguing that smaller open-source models are approaching frontier benchmark scores at 20 to 50 times lower cost. His point was that the long-term pattern may involve using a local model for 90% of tasks and deferring to frontier only when the output quality genuinely demands it.

Vercel

A hosting platform that lets you deploy a website in minutes, directly from a code repository. Claude Code builds the site; Vercel gets it live on a real URL. Ian Jones brought it up after building a complete site in about half an hour using exactly this combination. Wendy uses it for the fuel tracker on the Fourteen Seed site.

MLX

Apple's framework for running machine learning models natively on Apple Silicon chips (the M-series in modern Macs). Scott mentioned it when explaining how he runs his local Open Brain without sending anything to the cloud. If you're on a Mac with an M-chip and want to experiment with local models, this is the most efficient starting point.

Fine-tuning

Taking a general-purpose AI model and training it further on a specific dataset so it becomes a specialist in your domain. A fine-tuned small model can outperform a frontier model for a narrow task, at a fraction of the cost. Ryan Crabbe introduced this when arguing that small language models are fast becoming genuine alternatives to frontier models for the majority of everyday tasks.

Brownfield vs. greenfield

Terms borrowed from construction. Greenfield means building something new from scratch with no legacy constraints, AI-native from day one. Brownfield means implementing AI inside an existing business that already has systems, processes and people in place. Wendy introduced this framing when observing that the technical side of brownfield is often the easy part. The harder problem is the human side: habits, resistance, and the weight of how things have always been done.

Multi-agent

Running more than one AI agent at the same time, each handling a different part of a task. Rather than one AI doing everything in sequence, several work in parallel, coordinated from a central point. Wendy raised this after working with Anthropic's multi-agent tools in the CLI, and noted it is exactly the kind of setup that starts burning tokens fast if you are not paying attention.
