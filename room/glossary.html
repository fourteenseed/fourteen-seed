The glossary over time

Words that come up in our gatherings and deserve a plain-English explanation. Added after each session — because you should never have to nod along at something you don't quite follow.

Got a word that made you glaze over? Drop it in the WhatsApp group and it'll appear here.

21 May 2026 Gathering 2 · twelve terms

Token

The unit AI models use to measure and charge for work. Roughly a word, or part of one. When you're told to "watch your usage," this is what you're watching. At the Google keynote, tokens were mentioned more than AI itself — because for the companies building these tools, tokens are the product they're selling you.

API

Application Programming Interface — the way software talks to other software. When a business wants to use Claude inside their own product rather than having staff log in individually, they connect via the API. It's usage-based billing rather than a subscription, which is largely how Anthropic makes money from enterprise customers.

Vector store

A way of storing information so that an AI can search it by meaning rather than by exact words. If you ask "what did we discuss about costs?" a vector store finds relevant content even if no one used the word "costs" specifically. Think of it as a library organised by concept rather than by alphabetical title.

Knowledge graph

A way of organising information as a web of connected ideas rather than a flat list. Each concept is a node, and the connections between them are the relationships. It helps an AI understand not just facts but how they relate to each other — and lets you filter by how close something is to what you're actually working on right now.

Open Brain

A persistent memory layer that sits outside any single AI tool, so your context follows you wherever you go. Instead of re-introducing yourself every time you open a new chat, the AI connects to your Open Brain and already knows what you're working on and who you've spoken to. Popularised by Nate B. Jones; Wendy's is backed by Obsidian and Supabase, Scott's runs entirely on local hardware.

Local model

An AI model running on your own hardware rather than in a data centre somewhere. No API costs per query, data doesn't leave your machine, and you're not dependent on a provider's uptime or pricing. The trade-off: setup is more involved, and your hardware sets the ceiling on how capable the model can be.

Frontier model

The most capable, most expensive AI models at the cutting edge — Claude, GPT-4o, Gemini Ultra. As smaller open-source models close the gap fast, the long-term value of paying frontier prices is a live question. For now, they're what you reach for when the task needs the highest quality output.

Vercel

A hosting platform that lets you deploy a website in minutes, directly from a code repository. Claude Code can build the site; Vercel gets it live via a URL. Ian built a complete site in about half an hour using this combination. Wendy uses it for her fuel tracker on the Fourteen Seed site.

MLX

Apple's framework for running machine learning models natively on Apple Silicon chips (the M-series in modern Macs). It's what Scott uses to power his local Open Brain without sending anything to the cloud. If you're on a Mac with an M-chip and want to experiment with local models, this is the most efficient starting point.

Fine-tuning

Taking a general-purpose AI model and training it further on a specific dataset so it becomes a specialist in your domain. A fine-tuned small model can outperform a frontier model for a narrow task — at a fraction of the cost. Mentioned in the context of small language models becoming genuinely competitive alternatives.

Brownfield vs. greenfield

Terms borrowed from construction. Greenfield means building something new from scratch with no legacy constraints — AI-native from day one. Brownfield means implementing AI inside an existing business that already has systems, processes, and people in place. The technical side of brownfield is often the easy part; the human side is usually harder.

Multi-agent

Running more than one AI agent at the same time, each handling a different part of a task. Rather than one AI doing everything in sequence, several work in parallel, coordinated from a central point. Anthropic recently released multi-agent management tools in the CLI — the kind of thing that starts burning tokens fast if you're not paying attention.

Laura had finished Cowork and was already using it with a client — including running a project summary across a full folder of documents. Mark had completed his first two courses and was working through Cowork. Johnnie had been busy stepping down as Mayor and had Cowork lined up as his next course. Projects

  • Ryan is in the final stages of Flow Back, an app that lets product teams capture feedback through an AI agent, which then produces a PRD for the development team to act on. Launch target: Monday.
  • Johnnie developed the Table 41 concept in a Claude conversation — a restaurant table dedicated to solo diners, where others can join if they choose. He took the spec into Lovable and had a full website running in about 90 minutes. Claude also suggested a launch budget of £350,000, which he is treating as aspirational.
  • Ian built a complete website using Claude Code and Vercel in half an hour. His next project: a space education app where students play roles on Earth and in space, running science experiments remotely.
  • Wendy demonstrated how Cowork has changed her workflow — using it to update the AI room leaderboard via voice dictation, then committing to GitHub from the terminal.

Concepts discussed

  • Open Brain — Wendy explained the concept of a persistent memory layer that sits outside any single AI tool, letting context follow you across Gemini, Claude, and ChatGPT without re-introducing yourself each time. Scott demonstrated his version running entirely on local hardware.
  • Local models — Ian and Scott made the case for running AI on your own hardware (Apple Silicon via MLX) for cost, privacy, and data sovereignty. The question of backup strategy came up and was not fully resolved.
  • API vs. subscription — Ryan explained the difference between a personal Claude subscription and the API for enterprise use, including the PII and cost-tracking benefits of the API. Scott clarified that team and enterprise plans also exist for those who want cloud access without hitting the API directly.
  • Token efficiency — Wendy noted that running scheduled tasks before the US West Coast wakes up noticeably reduces costs and speeds up Claude. Worth remembering when setting up automations in Cowork.
  • Frontier vs. open source — Ryan raised the point that small language models are approaching frontier benchmark scores at 20–50x lower cost, and that the long-term model may involve using local models for 90% of tasks and deferring to frontier only when needed.
  • Brownfield vs. greenfield — Wendy observed a tension between building AI-native products (greenfield, fast) and implementing AI in existing businesses (brownfield, where the human side is often the harder problem).

Ideas from this session

The glossary idea came out of this gathering — Wendy suggested capturing unfamiliar terms after each session and building them into a page in the AI room. Members were invited to submit their top three words to the WhatsApp group.

Ryan raised the idea of a multiplayer Open Brain — a shared knowledge layer for a business (in his case, his wife's fitness gym), queryable by multiple people. Scott offered to help anyone exploring this.
