Exam readiness

The CCAF exam is no longer gated on ten people. If you have been building real things with Claude, you may be closer to sitting it than you think.

This page pulls together everything we know about preparing well: the official study route, the practice portal Antman shared from Nate’s group, and the readiness gate you want to clear before you book. The rule of thumb: courses plus real building, then calibrate with mock exams, then book. Don’t sit it on practice-exam scores alone.

Practice portal

The best tool we’ve found for exam prep is intellifyr.com/ccaf — shared by Antman via Nate B. Jones’s exec circle. It’s a free adaptive study system with 19 lessons, five domain drills, and a mock exam. It tracks your predicted score against 1000 and shows exactly where your blind spots are.

The daily routine is short: eight adaptive questions and one lesson, roughly 19 minutes. The mock exam is the important part — the readiness gate below uses mock scores, not lesson completion, as the signal you’re ready to book.

Readiness gate

Before booking the proctored exam, clear all three of these. Two out of three is not enough.

All three before you book
Two recent mock exams scored 900 or above (out of 1000). Not a one-off — two in a row, with a gap between them.
Blind-spot score 90%+ on both mocks. Questions you previously got wrong are now being answered correctly.
No domain below 80% on either mock. A single weak domain will cost you.
The practice exam tests recall in isolation. The real exam puts you inside production scenarios and asks for the right architectural call. Several candidates have scored full marks on the practice exam and then failed the live one, because they revised definitions instead of decisions. Courses plus real building are the preparation. The practice exam is final calibration — not a learning tool.

The five domains

Domains 1 and 3 together account for nearly half the exam. Domain 1 is the single heaviest and the most underestimated. Weight your time accordingly.

Domain Weight What it covers
D1 · Agentic architecture and orchestration 27% Agent loops, multi-agent coordinator and subagent patterns, task decomposition, hooks, session management
D2 · Tool design and MCP integration 18% Tool descriptions and boundaries, structured error responses, tool distribution, MCP server config
D3 · Claude Code configuration and workflows 20% CLAUDE.md hierarchy, slash commands, skills, plan mode, CI/CD integration
D4 · Prompt engineering and structured output 20% JSON schemas via tool use, few-shot, validation-retry loops, explicit criteria
D5 · Context management and reliability 15% Context preservation, escalation patterns, error propagation, provenance, human review

Suggested course sequence

Work in this order. Each phase builds on the one before. Times are rough guides: slower is fine if the material is sticking. Compiled from the cohort prep map, last updated 23 June 2026.

  • Phase 1 — Foundations (skip if experienced): Claude 101 and AI Fluency: Framework and Foundations. The baseline the exam quietly assumes. Roughly 3–5 hours.
  • Phase 2 — Building with the Claude API: The flagship course and the floor everything else stands on. Covers tool use, structured output, JSON schemas, context handling. Does the heavy lifting for domain 4 and underpins domains 1 and 2. Roughly 8–12 hours.
  • Phase 3 — Model Context Protocol: Introduction to MCP, then MCP: Advanced Topics. Build an actual MCP server, wire it up, and trigger tool errors on purpose. Domain 2 rewards people who have built, not just read. Roughly 12–16 hours.
  • Phase 4 — Subagents and agentic architecture: Introduction to Subagents, plus the Agent SDK material on loops and orchestration. This is domain 1 — the heaviest on the exam. Do not skip the building. Roughly 10–14 hours.
  • Phase 5 — Claude Code configuration and workflows: The Claude Code course plus CLAUDE.md, slash commands, skills, plan mode, and CI/CD material. Domain 3. Roughly 8–12 hours.
  • Phase 6 — Context management and reliability: No single course owns domain 5. It threads through the API course, Subagents, and the escalation and error-handling material. Treat this as targeted revision across what you have already done. Roughly 4–6 hours.
  • Phase 7 — Hands-on build, then calibrate: Build at least one real thing that exercises agents, tools, and structured output. Then, and only near the end, sit the mock exam.
Our cohort’s edge: we are already building real systems — Glassmark, Planory, the Morning Post rebuild, the planning application reader, and more. The hands-on grounding the exam actually rewards is something we have, not something we need to fake. Rough total: around 40 hours of structured study if you already have meaningful hands-on Claude experience, closer to 8 weeks part-time if you are building that experience as you go. The exam assumes roughly six months of real production work, so the building is not optional padding — it is the point.

Cohort checklist

  • Confirm CPN partner-company access on the Skilljar portal — our partner status should clear you straight through
  • Register for the exam and confirm current mechanics and any cost (free during the early-access window)
  • Work phases 1 to 6 in order, with heaviest time on domains 1 and 3
  • Build one real project that exercises agents, tools, and structured output
  • Use the practice portal to drill domains and find blind spots
  • Sit the mock exam late, as calibration — not as preparation
  • Clear all three readiness gate criteria, then book the proctored exam
