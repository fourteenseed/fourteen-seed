Hi, I'm Wendy. I run Fourteen Seed, an AI studio in Cornwall.

My background is in the visitor economy. Fifteen years co-founding and scaling Anytime Booking, a SaaS booking platform for outdoor hospitality, gave me deep roots in travel, tourism, and outdoor accommodation. We got to around £120m of bookings processed annually before I stepped back in 2025. I hold a NED seat there and I'm currently negotiating my full share exit, which I expect to complete at some point in the next six to twelve months. But my interests have moved with AI into something broader than any one sector. What I'm most drawn to now is AI implementation in the real world, and the effect AI is having on the surface of human interaction: where it's changing how people discover, decide, and connect, and where it isn't yet.

I'm putting this cohort together because I genuinely think six months of focused, peer-supported building is the fastest way through the Anthropic partner qualification, and because I want to be in a room of people who are doing the work, not just talking about it.

What I'm building

glassmark.ai. An AI visibility audit tool (AEO, answer engine optimisation) for visitor economy websites. Scans a site to assess how legible it is to AI agents and LLMs. Currently in private testing, moving toward go-to-market. This is the one holding most external interest right now and the faster path to revenue.

planory.ai. A B2B AI travel planning platform positioned as a digital Tourist Information Centre for the UK, focused initially on the South West. Connects travellers to local expert knowledge and helps destinations and accommodation providers surface in AI planning conversations. Currently in early B2B adoption with TICs and DMOs. Roughly half my attention.

The two are designed as complementary. glassmark assesses readiness. planory is the AI agent doing the discovering.

My husband James is also building under the Fourteen Seed umbrella. He has 25 years in professional kitchens and is building Gobbler.ai, a meal tracker and money-saving app for hospitality. I'm supporting rather than leading that one.

Top five skills

  1. B2B SaaS sales and commercial strategy. Fourteen years selling booking software to holiday parks, campsites, and glamping sites, from national businesses down to mum-and-pop operators. Before that I sold ice cream wholesale for Walls, which is where I first learned a sales funnel without realising that was what I was doing. I know how this industry buys, what makes it stall, and how to move it.
  2. Visitor economy domain knowledge. Tourism, hospitality, outdoor accommodation, destination marketing. I speak the language and I know the network.
  3. Product shaping. I'm not a developer, but I'm fluent enough to work directly with developers and AI agents. I've spent decades sitting next to builders, giving them the product vision, letting them build it, and then QA'ing what came back. That loop is what strengthens product shaping, and it's the same loop I now run with agents.
  4. Building in public and writing. I run Second Serve, a LinkedIn newsletter documenting the build journey. I write for a business audience that needs strategic depth, not just builder-to-builder chatter.
  5. Networking and relationship building. Strong connections across the South West and into the UK more broadly, including active pilot partners and board-level relationships. This is where a lot of glassmark's early traction comes from.

Where I'm weaker

Honest list: SQL queries and database schema work bore me rigid. I rely on agents and collaborators for deep technical implementation. I'm still learning how to design agent orchestration at scale, which is a big part of why I'm doing this cohort.

What's driving me to do this

Three things.

First, the honest version: my runway extends comfortably to around autumn 2026, but not much beyond that without Fourteen Seed generating real income. I need to be generating personal revenue before it runs out, which focuses the mind in a useful way. The goal isn't just to replace one salary. I want to build enough that James can leave his kitchen job and come into the studio full time, so we're both building.

I still hold a NED seat at Anytime Booking and retain my equity stake. I'm currently negotiating my full share exit, which I expect to complete at some point in the next six to twelve months. What that unlocks, for me and for everyone around me, is something I'm genuinely curious about. It changes the shape of what comes next, and I'm still figuring out what that looks like.

Second, I've been full-time in AI since August last year, and I've been lucky. The Nate B. Jones exec community has compressed what would have been years of solo reading into six months. Getting into a cohort of builders, working on real things against a real deadline, is the format that accelerates me further.

Third, selfishly, I want to align myself with great people building great things. That's the whole reason a cohort works. If the people in the room are serious, everyone moves faster.

What I can bring to the cohort

  • Alignment with everyone's individual learning path. We're all here to get through the Anthropic qualification together. I'm happy to share what good looks like, what's working, what isn't, and to swap notes on the actual build as we go.
  • Sales and commercial thinking for anyone who'd rather build than sell.
  • A strong South West UK network and beyond, across tourism, tech, and local government.
  • A bias to ship. I'd rather move than perfect.

What I'd like from the cohort

  • People who are genuinely in the building phase, not observers.
  • Honest critique. I'd rather hear "this won't work because" than polite agreement.
  • Cross-pollination between projects. If there's a natural referral or collaboration, let's take it.
  • A shared rhythm that gets us all through the Anthropic qualification together.

Outside the build

Right now I'm also researching my late father's amateur radio gear and writing a book about it. The hook is QSL cards: the physical confirmation cards that radio operators exchanged after making contact, one human leaving a trace for another across distance and static. The book is about the humans behind the technology and the humans in front of it, and what that surface of contact looks like when the medium changes.

The thread running through it is the transition from analogue to digital to AI: what stays human across each shift, what gets absorbed, and what gets lost. I'm genuinely curious about human interaction at the surface of real-world use cases, and where AI enters and changes that picture. Whether the book generates anything at some point is an open question. For now, it's one of the ways I'm figuring all of this out.
